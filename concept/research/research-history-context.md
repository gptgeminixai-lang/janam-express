SUMMARY:
All the core data plumbing for an India "birthday time machine" is free and confirmed working. The Wikimedia Feed "On This Day" API (tested live: GET https://api.wikimedia.org/feed/v1/wikipedia/en/onthisday/{all|births|deaths|events|holidays|selected}/{MM}/{DD}, also mirrored at https://en.wikipedia.org/api/rest_v1/feed/onthisday/...) needs no auth, returns CORS header access-control-allow-origin:*, and gives ~50 births per date with Wikipedia extracts and thumbnails — but it is NOT India-filtered (July 4 had only 1 Indian in 50) and Hindi (hi) is not a supported language (tested, 404). The right India filter is a Wikidata SPARQL query (tested live against https://query.wikidata.org/sparql, no auth, just a custom User-Agent): humans (P31=Q5) with citizenship India (P27=Q668) and birth date (P569) matching MONTH/DAY, ranked by wikibase:sitelinks as a fame proxy — for July 4 it instantly returned Gulzarilal Nanda, Cyrus Mistry, M.M. Keeravani, Yuki Bhambri, Chirag Shetty. World Bank API also tested live (needs -L to follow redirect): India 1985 population = 772,647,793 and crude birth rate = 35.42/1000, which derives to ~27.4M births that year ≈ ~75,000 Indians born the same day.

Weak spot: actual newspaper front pages. ProQuest Historical Newspapers has the full Times of India 1838–2012 page images but it is institutionally paywalled and not embeddable; TOI's own epaper only goes back a few days/months; Google News Archive (news.google.com/newspapers) is free and still up but carries almost no Indian papers and nothing past 2009 — so the practical play is to *describe* the day (via On This Day + hardcoded facts) rather than show scans. PM/President-by-date is tiny and fully hardcodeable (complete lists with exact dates returned below), and a rich 1950–2010 year→milestone table (liberalisation 24 July 1991, Pokhran-I 18 May 1974 / Pokhran-II 11 May 1998, first mobile call 31 July 1995 Jyoti Basu→Sukh Ram on Modi Telstra at Rs 8.40/min, VSNL public internet 15 Aug 1995, Delhi Metro 24 Dec 2002, Chandrayaan-1 22 Oct 2008, etc.) is included below with exact dates.

## Wikimedia 'On This Day' Feed API — free, no auth, CORS-enabled (tested live)
Endpoint: GET https://api.wikimedia.org/feed/v1/wikipedia/en/onthisday/{type}/{MM}/{DD} where type ∈ all | selected | births | deaths | events | holidays. Identical data also at https://en.wikipedia.org/api/rest_v1/feed/onthisday/{type}/{MM}/{DD}. Tested 2026-07-04: HTTP 200, content-type profile Specs/OnThisDay/0.5.0, header access-control-allow-origin:* (so callable directly from the browser), cache-control s-maxage=300. No API key needed; anonymous api.wikimedia.org traffic is limited (~500 req/hr/IP; 5,000/hr with a free personal API token), and the en.wikipedia.org/api/rest_v1 mirror asks for ≤200 req/s + a descriptive User-Agent. Response shape: {"births":[{"text":"<person, description>","year":1983,"pages":[{title, displaytitle, wikibase_item (Q-id!), thumbnail.source, originalimage, extract, extract_html, content_urls.desktop.page}]}], ...}. July 4 returned ~50 births, ~40 deaths, ~60 events. Caveats found by testing: (1) it is English-Wikipedia-centric — only 1 of 50 births on July 4 was Indian (Amol Rajan); (2) Hindi is NOT supported — /wikipedia/hi/onthisday/... returns 404 (supported langs: en, de, fr, sv, pt, ru, es, ar, bs, uk, tr, zh, it). So use it for the global 'what happened in the world' section, and use the wikibase_item Q-ids to cross-check India relevance, but do NOT rely on it for famous Indian birthdays.
SOURCE: https://api.wikimedia.org/feed/v1/wikipedia/en/onthisday/births/07/04 (docs: https://api.wikimedia.org/wiki/Feed_API/Reference/On_this_day, https://en.wikipedia.org/api/rest_v1/#/Feed)
WOW: One free GET call gives the user's exact birth date's world events, births and deaths, each with a Wikipedia thumbnail image and one-paragraph extract — instant rich content with zero scraping.

## Famous Indians sharing your birthday — Wikidata SPARQL, tested and it nails it
Endpoint: GET https://query.wikidata.org/sparql?format=json&query=... (no auth; MUST send a custom User-Agent header). Working query, tested live for July 4: SELECT ?person ?personLabel ?dob ?sitelinks WHERE { ?person wdt:P31 wd:Q5; wdt:P27 wd:Q668; wdt:P569 ?dob . FILTER(MONTH(?dob)=7 && DAY(?dob)=4) ?person wikibase:sitelinks ?sitelinks . SERVICE wikibase:label { bd:serviceParam wikibase:language "en". } } ORDER BY DESC(?sitelinks) LIMIT 10 — returned, ranked by fame (sitelink count): Gulzarilal Nanda (b. 4 Jul 1898, twice acting PM, 58 sitelinks), Yuki Bhambri (1992, tennis), M.M. Keeravani (1961, Oscar-winning composer), Cyrus Mistry (1968, Tata chairman), Chirag Shetty (1997, badminton). Key Q-ids/properties: Q5=human, P27=citizenship, Q668=India, P569=date of birth, P106=occupation (add for 'cricketer/actor' labels), P18=image (add OPTIONAL{?person wdt:P18 ?img} for photos). Add FILTER(YEAR(?dob)=1985) for exact same-DAY-and-YEAR matches ('born the very same day as you'). Limits: 60s query timeout, 5 parallel queries/IP, 60s query-time per minute per IP+UA — trivially fine if you pre-compute all 366 dates offline into JSON (recommended; the whole dataset is ~366 cheap queries). Alternative/backup: PetScan (petscan.wmcloud.org) intersecting enwiki Category:1985 births with Category:Indian people, but SPARQL is cleaner. Fallback ranking metric besides sitelinks: ?person wikibase:statements, or enwiki pageviews via https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/metrics/pageviews/per-article/en.wikipedia/all-access/all-agents/{title}/monthly/...
SOURCE: https://query.wikidata.org/sparql (limits: https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Wikidata:SPARQL_query_service/query_limits)
WOW: A user born 4 July learns they share a birthday with an acting Prime Minister of India, the Tata Group chairman, and the composer of 'Naatu Naatu' — with photos (P18) pulled free from Wikimedia Commons.

## India's population + births-per-day in your birth year — World Bank API, tested live
Endpoint (free, no auth, JSON): https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/IND/indicator/SP.POP.TOTL?format=json&date={YYYY} — NOTE: the server 302-redirects, so follow redirects (curl -L / fetch default). Tested for 1985: value = 772,647,793 (response: [{page,pages,per_page,total,lastupdated},[{indicator,country,date:"1985",value:772647793}]]). Crude birth rate: same URL with SP.DYN.CBRT.IN → 1985 = 35.42 per 1,000. Derived stat: births that year ≈ pop × CBR/1000 = 772.65M × 0.03542 ≈ 27.37M births → ÷365 ≈ ~75,000 Indians born the same DAY in 1985 (vs ~63,000/day in 2023 as birth rates fell to ~16.2). Other fun indicators, same URL pattern: SP.DYN.LE00.IN (life expectancy at birth — 1985 India: ~55.8 yrs, a killer 'you were expected to live to X' stat), SP.DYN.TFRT.IN (fertility rate, 4.4 in 1985), NY.GDP.PCAP.CD (GDP per capita — $296 in 1985). Range query: ?date=1960:2023 in one call. LIMITATION: World Bank series start at 1960; for 1950–1959 births use UN World Population Prospects Data Portal API (https://population.un.org/dataportalapi/api/v1, indicator 49 = total population, location 356 = India) or hardcode UN WPP values (India 1950 ≈ 357M, 1955 ≈ 395M). You can also cache all years in ONE call: https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/IND/indicator/SP.POP.TOTL?format=json&date=1950:2026&per_page=100.
SOURCE: https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/IND/indicator/SP.POP.TOTL?format=json (birth rate: SP.DYN.CBRT.IN; UN fallback: https://population.un.org/dataportalapi)
WOW: 'On the day you were born, roughly 75,000 other Indians were born with you — you are one of 772,647,793 Indians alive that year, and India has grown by 600 million people since' — real numbers, straight from a free API.

## Newspaper front pages of your birth date — mostly paywalled; here is what's actually usable
Reality check: (1) ProQuest Historical Newspapers: The Times of India 1838–2012 has full page-image PDFs of every issue including front pages, but it is institution-only (university library subscription), no public API, cannot be embedded — dead end for a consumer site. (2) TOI's own epaper (epaper.indiatimes.com) date-picker goes back only days-to-months; TOI has stopped public deep archives (their 2001-present TEXT article archive at timesofindia.indiatimes.com/archive.cms IS free — usable for 2001+ birthdays as headline text). (3) Google News Archive (https://news.google.com/newspapers) is free, alive (confirmed 2025/2026), browsable by title+date with PDF page scans, but content stops at 2009, keyword search is broken, and Indian coverage is thin (The Indian Express is the main Indian title there, patchy 1930s–1990s — deep-linkable, e.g. news.google.com/newspapers?nid=P9oYG7HA76QC). (4) British Newspaper Archive has ~41,000 pages of ToI (~27 years, mostly 1800s) — paywalled, irrelevant years. (5) Internet Archive (archive.org) has scattered ToI annuals and some Indian papers via Digital Library of India — not date-addressable. RECOMMENDATION for the product: don't promise scans; instead auto-compose a faux-vintage 'front page' from On This Day events + hardcoded milestones + Bollywood releases of that Friday, and for 2001+ birthdays link out to TOI's free text archive for that date. Wikipedia's year pages (e.g. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1985_in_India) are a goldmine of headline fodder per year, CC-licensed.
SOURCE: https://news.google.com/newspapers ; https://pq-static-content.proquest.com/collateral/media2/documents/hnp_india.pdf ; https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/archive.cms
WOW: Instead of a paywalled scan, the site generates a beautiful retro 'Front Page of {date}' mock newspaper from free data — which looks better than a blurry PDF anyway, and works for every date since 1950.

## Bonus implementation notes — pre-compute everything, three free APIs total
Architecture that came out of testing: (a) Pre-compute a static JSON per calendar date (366 files) offline from Wikidata SPARQL (famous-Indian birthdays, respect 5-parallel/60s-per-min limits with a User-Agent like 'BirthdayTimeMachine/1.0 (contact@...)'), so the live site never hits WDQS. (b) Call the On This Day feed client-side (CORS * confirmed) or cache it too — it's static historical data. (c) Cache all World Bank India series 1960–2026 in one request each (?date=1960:2026&per_page=100). (d) Photos: Wikidata P18 gives Commons filenames; render via https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/{filename}?width=300 (free, hotlink-friendly). (e) Day-of-week of birth (Mon/Tue...) is a free client-side JS Date computation that users love. (f) Bollywood angle: Wikidata SPARQL can also fetch films (P31=Q11424) with publication date (P577) in India around the birth date — 'the #1 movie the week you were born'. (g) For 'events in YOUR city', the On This Day pages array includes Q-ids; cross-reference coordinates (P625) against the birth place — sparse but occasionally magical.
SOURCE: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/ ; https://query.wikidata.org/sparql ; https://api.wikimedia.org/feed/v1
WOW: The entire mind-blowing page can be built from exactly three free, keyless, CORS-friendly APIs (Wikimedia Feed, Wikidata SPARQL, World Bank) plus two small hardcoded tables — zero running cost, no scraping, no paywalls.
